🔋 Micro Lesson: Deep Cycle vs Cranking Batteries

Two Different Jobs — Two Different Designs

Not all batteries are built the same. The internal plate design determines what a battery is good at — and using the wrong type for the application causes premature failure.

Cranking Battery (SLI)

Starting, Lighting, Ignition

  • Many thin plates = large surface area
  • Delivers massive current for a very short burst
  • Immediately recharged by alternator
  • Never meant to be deeply discharged
  • Used in: Cars, trucks, motorcycles

Deep Cycle Battery

  • Fewer, thicker plates
  • Delivers moderate current over a long period
  • Designed to be discharged 50-80% repeatedly
  • Much higher cycle life when deeply discharged
  • Used in: RVs, boats, golf carts, solar storage
Simple version: Cranking batteries sprint. Deep cycle batteries marathon. Using a sprinter for a marathon — or vice versa — destroys the battery quickly.

Why This Matters in the Shop

You will occasionally see customers who have installed the wrong battery type — usually someone who put a deep cycle battery in their car thinking "more is better." Understanding why this causes problems helps you explain it to the customer.

  • Deep cycle in a car: Thicker plates mean less surface area — lower CCA than rated, poor cold weather starts, charging system may not fully recharge it on short trips
  • Cranking battery in a deep cycle application: Thin plates get damaged quickly when deeply discharged — the battery fails within months
  • Dual purpose batteries: A compromise design that handles both reasonably well — common in RVs and boats that need engine starting AND house power
⚠ Shop tip: When a customer with an RV, boat, or camper van complains of repeated battery failures, always ask what type of battery they are using. A cranking battery in a house power application will never last — no matter the brand.
AGM deep cycle batteries are increasingly common in modern vehicles with high accessory loads — they combine the deep cycle capability with the sealed AGM construction for reliability.

Battery Ratings — Knowing What You're Looking At

Battery labels contain critical information. Knowing what the ratings mean helps you verify the correct replacement and understand a battery's condition.

  • CCA (Cold Cranking Amps) — amps the battery can deliver at 0°F for 30 seconds while maintaining 7.2V. The primary rating for cranking batteries. Higher CCA = better cold weather starting.
  • CA (Cranking Amps) — same test at 32°F. Always higher than CCA — don't confuse them.
  • RC (Reserve Capacity) — minutes the battery can deliver 25A at 80°F before dropping below 10.5V. Important for deep cycle applications.
  • Ah (Amp Hours) — total energy storage capacity. Common on deep cycle batteries. A 100Ah battery can deliver 5A for 20 hours.
For load testing: The standard load test applies half the CCA rating for 15 seconds. A 600 CCA battery gets loaded at 300A. If voltage stays above 9.6V at 70°F — the battery passes.
