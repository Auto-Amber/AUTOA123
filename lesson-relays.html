What is a Relay?

A relay is an electrically operated switch. A small control circuit uses low current to switch a high-current circuit on or off. Think of it like a light switch in your house — a small movement of your finger controls a large amount of power.

Key Term: Relay — an electromagnetic switch that uses a small control current to open or close a high-current circuit.

Relays protect switches and wiring from high current loads. Instead of running 30 amps through a small dashboard switch, the switch triggers the relay which handles the heavy current.

Relay Terminal Identification

Most automotive relays are ISO mini relays with 4 or 5 terminals. Knowing the terminal numbers is critical for testing and replacement.

Control (Coil) Terminals

  • 85 — Coil ground
  • 86 — Coil power (12V signal)

Switch (Load) Terminals

  • 30 — Common (battery power in)
  • 87 — Normally Open (NO) — closes when energized
  • 87a — Normally Closed (NC) — opens when energized
⚠ Shop tip: Most relays are normally open — terminal 87 only gets power when the coil is energized. If you're testing a relay, apply 12V to 86 and ground 85, then check for continuity between 30 and 87.

Where Are Relays Used?

Relays are everywhere in modern vehicles. Any time a computer or small switch needs to control a high-current load, there's likely a relay involved.

  • Cooling fan relay — ECM controls the fan without running high current through the ECM
  • Fuel pump relay — powers the fuel pump on engine start signal
  • Starter relay — ignition switch signal triggers high current to starter solenoid
  • A/C compressor relay — clutch engagement controlled through relay
  • Horn relay — small horn button triggers relay for horn power
Remember: If a high-current device isn't working, check the relay first — it's one of the most common and easiest fixes. Swap test with an identical relay before condemning anything else.
