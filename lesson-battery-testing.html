🔋 Micro Lesson: Battery Load Testing

Why Load Testing?

A battery can show a good open circuit voltage and still fail under load. A battery that reads 12.6V sitting still might drop to 8V the moment the starter motor asks it for 200 amps. That's why load testing is the most important battery test you can perform.

Think of it like this:
A person can look perfectly healthy standing still. But ask them to run a sprint and you'll find out fast whether their heart and lungs are actually up to the job.

The load test is the sprint. OCV is just standing still.
Key Term: Load Test — applies a controlled electrical load (½ the battery's CCA rating) for 15 seconds while monitoring voltage. The battery must stay at or above 9.6V to pass.
⚠ Before you load test: The battery must be at least 75% charged (12.4V or higher OCV). Testing a discharged battery gives inaccurate results and can damage the battery further.

The CCA Formula

The load you apply during a load test is always ½ of the battery's CCA rating. You find the CCA rating printed on the battery label.

Apply the load for exactly 15 seconds while watching the voltmeter. At the end of 15 seconds, read the voltage before releasing the load.

  • 9.6V or above = Battery passes ✓
  • Below 9.6V = Battery fails — recommend replacement
⚠ Temperature matters: The 9.6V threshold assumes testing at or above 70°F. In cold weather the minimum voltage drops slightly — check your tester's temperature compensation chart.

Load Test Step by Step

Using your carbon pile tester:

  • 1. Connect the tester clamps — red to positive, black to negative
  • 2. Check OCV first — must be 12.4V or higher before proceeding
  • 3. Set the load to ½ the battery's CCA rating
  • 4. Apply the load and start your 15-second count
  • 5. Read the voltage at 15 seconds — do not release the load early
  • 6. Release the load and record your result
  • 7. Let the battery rest 2–3 minutes before any additional testing
Document everything: On your lab sheet you should record the CCA rating, the load you applied, the voltage at 15 seconds, and pass or fail. A tech who writes "battery tested OK" without numbers is not doing their job.
Pass vs. Fail at 9.1V:
If your voltage drops to 9.1V at 15 seconds — that battery has failed. It is below the 9.6V minimum. Do not let a customer drive away on a battery that failed a load test. That's a comeback and a safety issue waiting to happen.
