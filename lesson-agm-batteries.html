🔋 Micro Lesson: AGM Batteries

What is an AGM Battery?

AGM stands for Absorbent Glass Mat. Instead of liquid electrolyte sloshing around inside, the electrolyte is absorbed into fiberglass mat separators between the plates. This makes the battery sealed, spill-proof, and significantly more capable than a traditional flooded battery.

Traditional Flooded Battery

  • Liquid electrolyte
  • Vented caps
  • Can spill if tipped
  • Slower recharge rate
  • Lower cycle life

AGM Battery

  • Electrolyte absorbed in mat
  • Fully sealed — no vents
  • Spill and vibration proof
  • Accepts charge 5x faster
  • 2-3x longer cycle life
Where you'll find AGM: Start-stop vehicles, luxury cars (BMW, Mercedes, Audi), vehicles with high electrical demands, and any vehicle where the battery is in the trunk or cabin rather than the engine bay.

Why AGM Must Be Charged Differently

This is the most important thing to understand about AGM batteries in the shop. Charging an AGM with a standard flooded battery charger can permanently damage or destroy it.

  • Flooded chargers use a high voltage finishing charge (15.5V+) to gas off and mix the electrolyte — AGM cannot vent this gas and will overheat and fail
  • AGM requires a lower, regulated voltage ceiling — typically 14.4–14.8V max during charging
  • Always use a charger with an AGM mode or a smart charger that detects battery type
  • Never fast charge an AGM — slow, controlled charging is essential
🚫 Shop rule: Before connecting ANY charger, identify the battery type. Flooded charger on an AGM = ruined battery and a comeback. Always check the label — AGM batteries are clearly marked.
⚠ Testing note: AGM batteries also require a conductance tester (like the GR8) rather than a traditional load tester. A carbon pile load test on a discharged AGM will give a false bad result.

Identifying and Handling AGM in the Shop

Knowing how to identify an AGM battery before you touch it is a critical shop habit. Here is what to look for:

  • Label: Will say AGM, Absorbent Glass Mat, or VRLA (Valve Regulated Lead Acid)
  • No caps: No removable vent caps on top — fully sealed case
  • Location: Often mounted in the trunk, under a seat, or in the cabin — flooded batteries are almost never placed inside the vehicle
  • Weight: Heavier than a comparable flooded battery
  • Vehicle label: Many vehicles have a sticker near the battery or in the hood stating AGM required
Replacement rule: If the vehicle came with AGM, it must be replaced with AGM. Installing a flooded battery in an AGM application will cause charging system faults, premature failure, and potentially damage the BCM or charging system.
