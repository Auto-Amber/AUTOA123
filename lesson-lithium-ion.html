Where Lithium Ion Shows Up in Vehicles

Lithium ion batteries are showing up in more vehicles every year. Understanding where they are used — and where they are NOT — is critical before you touch anything in the shop.

  • 12V lithium auxiliary batteries — used in some performance and European vehicles (Porsche, BMW, some Ford performance models) as a lightweight replacement for the traditional 12V lead-acid battery
  • Hybrid traction batteries — the large high-voltage pack that powers the electric drive motor (Toyota, Honda, Ford hybrids)
  • EV traction batteries — the primary energy source in fully electric vehicles (Tesla, Chevy Bolt, Nissan Leaf)
  • Mild hybrid 48V systems — a small lithium pack used to assist the engine and power accessories
For this course we focus on the 12V lithium auxiliary battery — the one that replaces the standard lead-acid battery. High voltage traction batteries are a separate specialty area covered in hybrid/EV training.

What Makes Lithium Different

A 12V lithium battery looks similar to a lead-acid battery from the outside but behaves very differently. Knowing the differences protects you, the vehicle, and your equipment.

  • Weight: Dramatically lighter — a lithium battery can weigh 60-70% less than an equivalent lead-acid battery
  • Voltage curve: Lithium holds a very flat voltage curve until nearly depleted — a standard battery tester will read it as fully charged even when it is almost dead
  • Charging: Requires a lithium-specific charger — standard lead-acid chargers will overcharge and damage it, potentially causing thermal runaway
  • Temperature sensitivity: Cannot be charged below freezing — has a built-in BMS (Battery Management System) that disconnects charging in cold conditions
  • Jump starting: Cannot be used as a donor battery to jump start another vehicle — the current draw can damage the lithium battery instantly
🚫 Never jump start FROM a lithium battery. Never charge a lithium battery with a standard lead-acid charger. Both can cause permanent damage or thermal runaway — a dangerous condition where the battery overheats uncontrollably.

Identifying and Testing Lithium in the Shop

Before testing or charging any battery, always identify the chemistry. Here is what to look for with lithium:

  • Label: Will say Lithium, LiFePO4 (lithium iron phosphate), or Li-ion
  • Weight: Noticeably lighter than expected for the size
  • No standard terminals: Some lithium batteries use different terminal configurations or have a BMS connector
  • Service information: Always check the OEM service manual before testing — the procedure for lithium is different
⚠ Testing lithium: Standard conductance testers (including the GR8) are NOT calibrated for lithium chemistry. You need a lithium-compatible tester or follow OEM diagnostic procedures. A standard test will give you meaningless results.
Bottom line shop habit: Identify before you touch. Lead-acid, AGM, EFB, and lithium all look similar from a distance and all require different handling. Check the label every time — no exceptions.
