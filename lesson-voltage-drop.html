🔋 Micro Lesson: Voltage Drop Testing

What is Voltage Drop?

Voltage drop is the loss of voltage across a resistance in a circuit. In a perfect world, a wire or connector has zero resistance — all the voltage gets delivered to the load with nothing wasted. In the real world, corrosion, loose connections, and damaged cables all add resistance that steals voltage from the circuit.

Garden hose analogy again:
Imagine a garden hose with a kink in it. Water pressure at the spigot is fine, but pressure at the nozzle is low. The kink is stealing the pressure. A corroded battery cable does the same thing — the battery voltage is fine, but less of it reaches the starter motor.
Key Rule: Voltage drop testing must be performed with the circuit under load — current must be flowing. At rest (no current), every connection looks perfect. The problem only shows up when electrons are actually trying to move through the resistance.

This is why Technician B in the quiz was wrong. Testing with the circuit at rest gives you near-zero readings on everything — you'll never find the problem.

The 0.1V Rule and DVOM Setup

The acceptable maximum voltage drop across any single connection or cable in a battery circuit is 0.1V (100 millivolts). More than that and you have excess resistance that needs attention.

DVOM setup for voltage drop:

  • Set to DC Volts
  • Place the red lead at the power source side of the connection
  • Place the black lead at the load side of the connection
  • The reading tells you how much voltage is being lost at that connection
⚠ For battery cables: Test while cranking (engine cranking, not running) for maximum load. Have a helper crank while you watch the meter — or use a remote starter switch.

What High Voltage Drop Tells You

Finding high voltage drop on a battery cable tells you there is resistance somewhere in that cable or connection. Common causes:

  • Corroded battery terminal or post
  • Loose battery clamp — not fully tightened
  • Damaged cable — internally corroded, frayed, or undersized for the circuit
  • Poor ground connection at the chassis or engine block
  • Aftermarket add-on devices spliced into the battery cables
Don't just clean the terminal: If you find 0.4V drop across the positive cable end, cleaning the visible corrosion off the terminal might fix it — or the cable itself might be corroded internally for its entire length. Test the full cable end to end, not just the connection point.
Think like a tech, not a parts changer:
High drop at the positive post-to-terminal = clean or replace the terminal clamp.
High drop end-to-end on the cable = the cable has internal damage and needs replacement.
High drop on the ground side = find the ground connection point at the chassis and inspect it. A bad ground affects everything on that circuit.
