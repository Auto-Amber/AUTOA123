📋 Micro Lesson: Repair Order Documentation

The Repair Order is a Legal Document

In Washington state, a technician can be held personally liable for work they perform. The repair order is not just a record of what you did — it is a legal document that can be used as evidence in a lawsuit against you, the shop, or both.

Real scenario:
A customer brings in a car that won't start. You test the battery, replace it, and write "battery replaced — OK." Two weeks later the car won't start again. The customer claims you didn't properly diagnose the problem. Your repair order says nothing about what you tested, what you found, or what specs you used.

You have no defense. The paper says almost nothing.

Detailed documentation is your protection.

Beyond liability, a well-written repair order serves three other people:

  • The service advisor — needs to explain the work to the customer in plain terms
  • The next technician — picks up where you left off without starting over
  • The warranty department — needs specific measurements to approve a claim
⚠ Bottom line: Vague notes protect nobody. Specific, measured, documented steps protect everyone — including you.

The Format — From Concern to Cause

Every repair order follows the same structure. Think of it as telling a complete story — beginning, middle, and end.

Step 1 — Verify the customer concern
Always verify first. Never skip to testing without confirming the complaint is real and reproducible.

Step 2 — Document what you found at each step
Every test gets its own line. Every line includes: what you did, the measurement, the unit, the spec, and OK or NG.

Step 3 — State the root cause
One clear sentence identifying what failed and why.

Step 4 — Recommendation and labor time
What needs to be done, what part is needed, and the flat rate time for the repair.

The notation system for each test line:

Format Performed [test] — [measurement] [unit] (spec: [specification]) [OK / NG]

Applied to battery testing:

Examples Checked OCV — 12.4V (spec: 12.4V min) ✓ OK
Performed load test — 9.1V at 15 sec (spec: 9.6V min) ✗ NG — FAIL
Checked positive cable voltage drop — 0.08V (spec: 0.1V max) ✓ OK
Checked negative cable voltage drop — 0.38V (spec: 0.1V max) ✗ NG — FAIL
⚠ Units matter: "12.4" means nothing. "12.4V" is a measurement. Always include the unit — volts, amps, ohms, minutes, psi. Every time. No exceptions.

Weak vs Professional — Side by Side

Same battery job. Same findings. Very different documentation. Read both and ask yourself which tech you'd want working on your car — and which one you'd want to be if this ended up in court.

⚠ Weak — Do Not Write Like This Verified customer concern: battery issues

Checked battery — bad
Replaced battery
Tested OK

Recommend: new battery
Labor: 0.5 hr
✓ Professional — Write Like This Verified customer concern: vehicle requires jump start after sitting overnight

Checked battery OCV — 12.1V (spec: 12.4V min) NG
Charged battery to 12.6V — proceeded with load test
Performed load test 300A / 15 sec — 9.1V (spec: 9.6V min) NG — FAIL
Checked positive cable voltage drop — 0.06V (spec: 0.1V max) OK
Checked negative cable voltage drop — 0.07V (spec: 0.1V max) OK
Scanned for DTCs — none present
Checked for TSBs — none applicable

Root cause: Battery failed load test — internal plate degradation consistent with age and discharge history

Recommendation: Replace battery with OEM spec Group 35 / 550 CCA
Parts: 1x battery Group 35 550CCA
Labor: 0.5 hr
Notice: The professional version tells the complete story — what was found, what spec it was compared to, what passed, what failed, and exactly why the battery is being replaced. If this job came back, you have every measurement documented. If it went to court, you have a defense.
