🔋 Micro Lesson: Sulfated Batteries

What is Sulfation?

Sulfation is the most common cause of premature battery failure. It happens when a battery is left in a discharged or partially discharged state for an extended period of time.

Inside a lead-acid battery, the plates are made of lead and lead dioxide. The electrolyte (sulfuric acid and water) reacts with the plates to produce electricity. When the battery discharges, lead sulfate crystals form on both plates — this is normal. When the battery is recharged, those crystals dissolve back into the electrolyte.

Sulfation occurs when the battery stays discharged long enough that those lead sulfate crystals harden and grow larger. Once hardened, they cannot be dissolved by normal charging — they permanently reduce the battery's capacity.
Think of it like limescale in a pipe:
A little mineral buildup rinses away easily. But if you let it sit and harden for months, it narrows the pipe permanently. Sulfation is the same idea — temporary crystals become permanent damage.

How Sulfation Happens

Sulfation builds up fastest in these situations:

  • Vehicle sits unused for weeks or months
  • Battery is repeatedly run down and only partially recharged (short trips, lots of accessories)
  • Battery is stored without a maintenance charger
  • Vehicle has a parasitic draw that slowly discharges the battery overnight
  • Battery was installed with insufficient CCA for the vehicle's demands
⚠ Shop reality: A customer who says "my battery keeps dying" and has been jumping it for two months likely has a sulfated battery AND a parasitic draw. Fix both — a new battery in a car with a draw will sulfate again fast.

Diagnosing and Dealing With Sulfation

A sulfated battery often shows these symptoms:

  • Won't fully charge — voltage plateaus early during charging
  • Fails load test even after charging
  • Gets warm quickly during charging (internal resistance is high)
  • Low specific gravity readings that don't improve after charging
Can sulfation be fixed? Early, soft sulfation can sometimes be reversed with a slow desulfation charge cycle using a smart charger. Hard sulfation — where the crystals have fully crystallized — cannot be reversed. The battery needs replacement.

As a technician, your job is to:

  • 1. Charge the battery fully before testing
  • 2. Perform a load test — if it fails after a full charge, the battery is likely sulfated
  • 3. Diagnose and fix the root cause (parasitic draw, short trips, wrong battery spec)
  • 4. Recommend the correct replacement battery for the vehicle
Don't just sell the battery:
If the root cause isn't fixed, the new battery will fail the same way. A tech who diagnoses the reason behind the sulfation — and communicates it to the customer — is the tech who earns trust and avoids comebacks.
