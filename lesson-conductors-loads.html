The Basic Circuit: Source, Conductor, Load, Ground

Every complete electrical circuit has four essential parts. Understanding each one is the foundation of all electrical diagnosis.

  • Source — provides voltage (battery, alternator)
  • Conductor — carries current to the load (wires, connectors, switches)
  • Load — converts electrical energy into work (light, heat, motion)
  • Ground — completes the return path back to the source
Remember: Current can only flow in a complete circuit. Any break — open wire, bad connector, missing ground — stops the whole thing.

Conductors — Moving Current Efficiently

A conductor is any material that allows electrons to flow easily. In automotive wiring, conductors are the wires, connectors, fusible links, and switches that carry current to the load.

Good Conductors

  • Copper wire
  • Aluminum wire
  • Vehicle body/frame (ground path)
  • Relay contacts

Resistance Adds Up

  • Corroded connectors
  • Loose terminals
  • Undersized wire gauge
  • Damaged insulation (shorts)
⚠ Shop tip: A conductor should have near-zero resistance and near-zero voltage drop. If you find more than 0.2V drop across a wire or connector under load, that conductor has excessive resistance and needs attention.

Loads — Where the Work Happens

A load is any component that converts electrical energy into another form of energy. Loads are intentional resistance — they are designed to use voltage and current to do something useful.

  • Light (photons): Headlamps, interior lights, LEDs, instrument cluster
  • Heat: Rear defroster, heated seats, glow plugs
  • Motion: Motors (fans, wipers, windows, starters, fuel pumps)
  • Sound: Horns, speakers
  • Magnetism: Solenoids, relay coils, injector coils
Key diagnostic principle: Voltage is used up (dropped) across loads, not conductors. If you find significant voltage drop across a wire or connector — that conductor has become an unintended load. That is a problem.

A load that is open (broken internally) will show full source voltage on one side and zero on the other. A shorted load shows zero voltage drop across it — all voltage is dropped somewhere else.
