What is Amperage?

Amperage (amps) is the measurement of how much current is flowing through a circuit. If voltage is the pressure pushing electrons, amperage is the volume of electrons actually moving.

Garden hose analogy:
Voltage = water pressure at the spigot
Amperage = how much water is actually flowing through the hose
Resistance = how kinked or narrow the hose is
Key Term: Ampere (A) — the unit of electrical current. One amp = 6.24 billion billion electrons passing a point per second.

In automotive work, you'll see everything from milliamps (mA) for parasitic draw tests all the way to hundreds of amps for starter motor circuits.

Ohm's Law and Amperage

Amperage is directly tied to voltage and resistance through Ohm's Law:

This means:

  • If voltage goes up and resistance stays the same → current increases
  • If resistance goes up and voltage stays the same → current decreases
  • A short circuit (near zero resistance) causes extremely high current flow
⚠ Shop tip: High resistance in a circuit (corroded connector, bad ground) causes low amperage — and the component won't work correctly. This is why voltage drop testing matters.

Amperage in the Shop

Knowing typical amperage values helps you quickly identify problems. Here are common reference points:

  • Parasitic draw (normal): 20–50 mA (milliamps)
  • Parasitic draw (concern): over 50 mA
  • Headlight circuit: 8–15 A per lamp
  • Cooling fan motor: 15–30 A
  • Fuel pump: 4–8 A
  • Starter motor: 100–250 A (cranking)
  • Alternator output: 60–200 A depending on load
Testing amperage: Your DVOM measures amps in series — the meter must be part of the circuit path. Never connect an ammeter in parallel — it will blow the fuse in your meter or damage the circuit.
