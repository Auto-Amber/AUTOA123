🔋 Micro Lesson: Battery State of Charge

What is State of Charge?

State of charge (SOC) tells you how much energy is left in a battery — similar to a fuel gauge. A battery at 100% SOC is fully charged. A battery at 0% is completely discharged and may be damaged.

Think of it like a fuel tank:
Full tank = 100% SOC = ~12.6V or higher
Half tank = 50% SOC = ~12.2V
Empty tank = 0% SOC = below 11.8V

Just like you can't do a proper fuel system test on an empty tank, you can't accurately load test a battery that isn't sufficiently charged.
Key Term: Open Circuit Voltage (OCV) — battery voltage measured with no load and after the battery has rested for at least 2 hours. This gives you the most accurate SOC reading from a DVOM.

There are three ways to test SOC: open circuit voltage, hydrometer, and the built-in sight glass (eye). Each method has its place depending on what's in front of you.

Method 2: Hydrometer
Draws electrolyte from a cell and measures specific gravity — only works on flooded/wet cell batteries with removable caps. A fully charged cell reads 1.265–1.280. More than 0.050 difference between cells = weak cell.

⚠ Important: Hydrometer testing does NOT work on AGM or gel cell batteries — they are sealed. Use OCV instead.

The Built-In Sight Glass (Eye)

Many maintenance-free batteries have a built-in hydrometer — a small colored dot or window on the top of the battery case. It gives you a quick visual SOC indicator without any tools.

Remember: The sight glass only shows one cell's condition. A green eye does not guarantee the battery is good — you still need to perform a proper load test before condemning or clearing a battery.
⚠ Shop tip: A clear or light yellow sight glass means the electrolyte level is too low to read. This battery likely needs replacement — do not attempt to charge or load test it.
