🔋 Micro Lesson: Battery Ratings

Why Ratings Matter

Every battery has a label with several ratings on it. These numbers aren't just marketing — they tell you exactly what the battery can and can't do. Putting the wrong battery in a vehicle can mean a no-start on a cold morning, a short battery life, or a comeback from an unhappy customer.

There are four ratings you need to know:

Most important for your work: CCA and RC. CCA tells you about cold-start ability. RC tells you how long the battery can keep the car running if the charging system fails.

CCA and CA — Cold and Warm Cranking

CCA (Cold Cranking Amperes) — The number of amps a battery can deliver for 30 seconds at 0°F while maintaining at least 7.2V. This is the most important rating for cold-climate starting.

Why cold matters:
Cold temperatures do two things at once — they slow down the chemical reactions inside the battery (reducing output) AND make the engine oil thicker (requiring MORE cranking power). That's a double hit on the battery when it's already at its weakest.

CA (Cranking Amperes) — Same test but at 32°F instead of 0°F. Because it's a warmer test condition, CA numbers are always higher than CCA for the same battery. You'll see CA on batteries sold in warmer climates where sub-zero temperatures aren't a concern.

⚠ Don't mix up CCA and CA: A battery labeled "600 CA" is NOT the same as "600 CCA." The CA rating is easier to achieve. Always verify you are meeting the OEM CCA spec, not just the CA spec.
2 / 4

RC and AH — How Long Can It Last?

RC (Reserve Capacity) — The number of minutes a fully charged battery can deliver 25 amps before dropping below 10.5V. This tells you how long the vehicle can run on battery power alone if the alternator fails.

RC in the real world: A battery with 90 minutes RC means if your alternator dies on the highway, you theoretically have 90 minutes to get to a shop — assuming moderate electrical load. In practice, headlights, AC, and other loads shorten that window.

AH (Ampere Hour) — Total energy capacity over a long, slow discharge. A 50AH battery can deliver 5 amps for 10 hours, or 1 amp for 50 hours. Used more in deep-cycle applications (RVs, marine, AGM backup systems) than standard automotive.
